Dr. APJ Abdul Kalam 1931-2015 "Dream Dream Dream. Dream transform into thoughts and thoughts result is action."
Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam was born on October 15, 1931, in Rameswaram, a small town in Tamil Nadu, India. Coming from a humble background, he grew up in a simple household and supported his family from a young age by distributing newspapers. Despite financial struggles, he remained a bright and hardworking student, eventually studying physics and aerospace engineering. His determination and love for learning laid the foundation for an extraordinary career.

Missile Man of India


Dr. Kalam began his career at the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) and later moved to the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO). He played a key role in the development of India's first satellite launch vehicle (SLV-III), which successfully placed the Rohini satellite in orbit in 1980. He was also a driving force behind the Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme (IGMDP), which led to the development of missiles like Agni and Prithvi, earning him the nickname “Missile Man of India.”

Role in Nuclear Program


One of Dr. Kalam's most significant contributions was his involvement in India's nuclear tests at Pokhran in 1998. As the Chief Scientific Adviser to the Prime Minister, he played a crucial role in making India a nuclear power. These tests marked a turning point in India's strategic defense capabilities and global standing.

Author and Visionary Thinker


Dr. Kalam was not only a scientist and leader but also a prolific writer and thinker. He authored several inspirational books, including Wings of Fire (his autobiography), Ignited Minds, India 2020, and My Journey. Through his writings, he shared his dreams for a developed India and emphasized the importance of education, innovation, and national development.

Awards and Honors


In recognition of his service to the nation, Dr. Kalam received numerous awards, including the Padma Bhushan (1981), Padma Vibhushan (1990), and Bharat Ratna (1997), which is India's highest civilian award. He also received honorary doctorates from over 40 universities worldwide.

Legacy and Death


Dr. Kalam's legacy continues to inspire millions across India and the world. He was deeply committed to the progress of the nation and the empowerment of its youth. On July 27, 2015, while delivering a lecture at the Indian Institute of Management in Shillong, he collapsed and passed away doing what he loved most—teaching and inspiring. His life remains a powerful reminder that with hard work, determination, and a strong sense of purpose, anyone can rise to greatness.
